Cyberpunk 2077 might have had one of the most unfortunate releases of a video game to date. It's almost as if it was fighting tooth and nail with No Man's Sky for the most controversial launch they could muster. Back in 2020, this game was a STATE on consoles. I initially played through it on the Xbox One and somehow enjoyed it. The game ran at probably a sub-20 frames per second and had more crashes than a demolition derby. It had a disappointing amount of bugs, and a lot of that promised role-playing experience (and just content in general) was just not there. It had the makings of a great game and a fantastic story, but it seemed to need more time in the oven than it already had.

But in 2023, the game finally had its big ‘revival’, so to speak. With its 2.0 update and Phantom Liberty DLC, it provided what was essentially a brand new experience. I’d been looking forward to it, and when it arrived, I bought the game again (for a third time) for PS5. TLDR: damn.

## The Story
---

The game is focused on V, a mercenary in Night City, trying to make a name for themselves. Night City is your typical futuristic, uber-dangerous city, so of course everyone is out to get you. When a specific job goes wrong, you find yourself stuck with a dead terrorist in your head and a ticking time bomb that will give you more than just a hangover. I won’t say much more, but what feels like a rather simple plot unfolds into a thrilling, fantastically written narrative experience that will test your morals and leave you shaken AND stirred.

In classic CD Projekt Red fashion, quite a lot of the game’s story is told through side quests (or jobs, as the game so calls it), making a lot of what makes the game shine possible to completely miss – and this quite literally includes meeting major characters and even whole endings. It’s for the most part quite obvious when you might be locked out of something, so it isn’t mandatory to do every side quest you are given immediately. And for those who hate tedious chores for side content – Cyberpunk 2077 offers nothing of the sort. Side quests have their own fleshed out plots and stories, some even going on through multiple missions. They are all varied enough to never get bored, and all flesh out the world of Cyberpunk just that bit more. It’s not like you don’t go rewarded too, some side missions offer some very interesting and unique rewards. Even the gigs, the smaller, less story focused missions are all fun to play through and provide carefully crafted encounters and locations to test your stills in different and distinct ways.

The main story itself is beautifully told. Each character is written fantastically, and your relationships with some of them are moulded by the choices you make.  Everyone has their own goals, beliefs and ambitions, and no one feels like they’re out of place or don’t belong in the world. The narrative is heart-wrenching and tear-jerking in all the right ways and will stick with you for quite some time after the credits finish rolling. And if you can’t let the game go yet – there’s plenty of different endings to explore.

## Choices Matter?
---

One of the most advertised parts of Cyberpunk 2077 were its choices and life paths. Corpo, Street Kid or Nomad, whichever you pick would shape your story – or so they said. Beyond a different first hour, those life paths never seem to have much of an impact other than allowing you to say different things in different conversations. They might shift the outcome of minor events, but your life path never seems to serve much of a purpose. Your choices themselves also largely lead to the same outcome story-wise, maybe with a couple characters liking you a little more or a little less.

 Where these choices do shine, though, is how you handle specific confrontations and situations. Talk it out with some intelligence and wit, give them a good old threat of violence, or just use the tried-and-true technique of unloading your weapon in their face - they’re all possible and all fun. That’s the kind of choice Cyberpunk 2077 gives you, and for me, it was… good enough. Would it have been nicer to have more agency in how things turn out in the story beyond a few more options or changed lines? Definitely. The only real choice that matters in Cyberpunk 2077 is the ending – and you’re not even locked into a specific one based on how you behave, and only a few are hidden away behind a couple of choices. While I really like the fact that there are plenty of endings to choose from, it would have been nicer for an RPG based on branching choices to give those choices more of an impact.

 ## Be Your Own Cyberpunk
 ---

 A slightly more abstract choice is how you play Cyberpunk 2077. The game has a huge set of skill trees and complex mechanisms to allow you to build your character exactly how you want them. And there aren’t enough skill points to unlock every single skill – you can be a jack of all trades, but a master of none. You can focus your skills on being a super high-speed melee ninja, a super cool ‘in the shadows’ assassin, an intelligent combat hacker, or a strong, brute force tank that takes things head on. Or you can mix them up a bit! I personally went for a high-speed reflex and cool build so I could zip in and out, taking down all the enemies before they even realise that I’m there. Feel free to judge me on that.

 You can also give your character cybernetic implants. Want strong arms that could probably punch someone into space? Absolutely. Arms that turn into blades to slice up scavs? Why not. Weird boosters on your legs to let you double jump? Definitely. Anything you need to give you the leg up in combat or traversal is at your disposal… if you can handle it (and pay for it). You can also upgrade your eyes for better scanning, your heart to revive you when you die, replace your whole spine and let yourself practically stop time if you want to. You can also upgrade your cyberdeck if you fancy going the smart, hacker route. I never did that because, let’s be honest, I don’t have the skillset for that. You can control cameras, cause distractions, explode objects, manipulate other people’s cyberware, whatever floats your boat. These options also have effects outside of combat, allowing you to take different approaches to literally anything – how to get into buildings or restricted areas, accessing information, even some conversations!

But it’s not just the number of options you have that make the gameplay so interesting. The combat itself is just GREAT. Guns feel weighty and great to control, and each one has different stats and traits that change not just its handling but functionality as well. Some weapons can shoot through walls, some have enhancements to allow them to ricochet, some just straight up cheat and auto-target your enemies. No weapon you pick up is exactly the same. The melee combat in the game also feels great to play, but a little less varied and developed. 

Situational awareness is paramount in combat – enemies are plentiful and can sometimes actually behave somewhat intelligently, especially on harder combat difficulties. They will take cover, flank you, even use hacks on you to make your life miserable. Knowing where to cover yourself and knowing how to use the objects around you will help shift the tides in your favour, *and* make you feel like a complete badass doing it.

## Live in the World
---

Sometimes, though, it’s a little difficult to concentrate in combat because the soundtrack is just too damn good. The music that plays during combat encounters or during stealth is just so fitting, very cyberpunk and really pumps you up. The radio in cars also feels right at home in the game’s world, and the music during story beats and emotional moments hits like a truck. They perfected the atmosphere of the world we are inhabiting, and in doing so made it feel so much more immersive.

The design of Night City itself is a marvel. Meticulously crafted, it might not be the largest open world in a game, but it feels the most dense. Its verticality is unparalleled, and they crammed detail into every last corner of the map. Its neon coat of paint is mesmerising to the point where sometimes you just want to go for a drive around to take in the sights – until you come across some gang warfare. The streets, the giant, blaring advertisements, the people, and the tucked away corners perfectly contrast the majesty of the bright lights and stunning vistas that the city has to offer, making it beautifully morbid. So many interiors to the world are accessible too, and the ability to purchase new apartments, it almost feels close enough to a world that you could live in.

## Verdict
---

Cyberpunk 2077 survived its tragic launch and came back to be what is quite possibly one of the greatest games of all time. With a tragic, gripping story, compelling performances, gorgeous visuals, a stellar soundtrack and addicting gameplay, it’s a game you really can’t pass up. Save for a couple of persisting bugs and a slightly underwhelming choices system, it’s one of the closest a game has even been to being perfect – and that’s a far cry from it’s original state.

*Game played on a base PS5 on Performance Mode*
