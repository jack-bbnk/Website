I slept on Persona 5 (Royal) for way too long. Admittedly, at the time, I only had a Nintendo Switch, so my first playthrough of the game was on there back in 2022. I’ve played the game through three times now, my most recent one being on PS5, in which I finally achieved 100% completion of the game – perhaps the first Persona fan to play their own games: definitely beating the larper allegations.

## A Masterpiece Narrative
---

Persona 5 offers one of the most in depth, interesting narratives that I have had the delight of experiencing. It’s not some simple ‘school kid finds alternate world and has to save the day’, it’s a surprisingly deep dive into serious and pressing topics such as abuse, depression, prejudice, politics and more. It’s not a dark game per se, but it does not take these themes lightly and explores them in such a way that it doesn’t poke fun or belittle them, and that’s something I really appreciate. The narrative feels rather simple to start with, but it doesn’t take long for the ball to start rolling, and it does NOT slow down for a second. Even if the pace of the narrative seems to be slowing down a little, something else will grab you by the collar and pull you in even further into the conspiracies. It’s a game I quite frankly couldn’t put down until it was done, and when it finally was, I wanted to pick it right back up again. I didn’t want to leave the world, the characters – I yearned for more, and not many games can really make me feel that way.

The structure of how Persona 5 Royal’s story is told also adds to its effortless charm. It starts in media res before throwing you right back to the beginning. The majority of the game is spent gradually building up to that point, uncovering the many mysteries and incredibly woven twists until you reach that fateful moment. It’s not the most unique method of storytelling, but it puts such a fresh twist on the concept and executes it in such a fantastic way that I honestly feel jealous as to how well it was written. Each step in the story felt like a meaningful evolution of the narrative, and it might be among the most satisfying jigsaws to piece together.

If there was one complaint I had to make about the story, it felt like it could have ended about three times. Each time it continued, it did explain every gap in the story, and the final overall conclusion was more than satisfactory, but it did make the pace of the story stutter for brief moments while it got back up to speed.


## Beautifully Written Characters
---

Every character you get introduced to, especially in regard to the Phantom Thieves (the main cast), feels different. No character is some basic rinse and repeat archetype and no one is there just to fill up time and space. They all have well thought out motives, personalities, perspectives and habits. They are all unique to one another and are each so charming – with some standouts being Makoto, Ryuji, Sojiro and Futaba. The dynamics between these characters also feels beautifully natural, the conversations and jokes flowing so smoothly that I don’t think another series of games has ever truly matched, at least on this scale with this large of a group. This wholesome and amusing dynamic between the team has made me smile and even chuckle on multiple occasions, and it’s elevated further by an incredible range of talented voice actors that breathe life into these characters. Vocal performance has always been a very important focal point for me in games, as they can deliver a depth of emotion that you didn’t even realise the medium could give you, which is why Persona 5 Royal strikes such a chord with me. A very small number of games has a cast where every single one knocks it out of the park – and the Persona series achieves this every time without fail. A good voice actor can often make a mediocre script sound good, so give a great cast some incredible, witty and profound writing and you get a recipe for not just success, but an experience that you won’t forget (I’m sounding like a proper snob now). Of course nothing is perfect, and I did find that there were some tropes, moments and voices clips that I did not like, as well as a small number of scenes that felt like their only purpose was fan service, and felt a little out of place, even weird. They aren’t long though and easy to look past with how insignificant they are, especially in a 100+ hour game, but that doesn’t mean I’ll completely ignore them.

Even the villains in this game are written well, though. There’s no black and white with most villains in this game, irrespective of if they are big or small. They aren’t ‘purely evil’, or just ‘misunderstood’, they are complex and unique, thought provoking and resonant. And that’s part of the overall theme of Persona 5 Royal: your justice. There is no set definition of what is right or wrong in this game, and the pursuit of doing what is right versus doing what is legal is what drives some of the most nuanced conflicts in the game. The Phantom Thieves follow their justice, but that doesn’t mean it’s perfect.


 ## Stylish Gameplay
 ---

I’ve gone on about how I love the messages, characters and acting in the game, but I haven’t actually mentioned how it plays. I’m not the best at JRPGs, in fact I would go as far to say as I straight up suck at them – but the combat of Persona 5 Royal is GREAT. It is beyond stylish and smooth, with an incredible level of depth and technicality  that makes it surprising it works so well. Even though there is so much to it, it’s never overbearing or hard to understand, and all the systems come naturally in gameplay. They’re taught at a comfortable pace, and once you know what you are doing, the floor opens up for some of the most diverse and fluid combinations I’ve played in a turn-based game. Striking enemies’ weaknesses to chain Baton Passes not only let you unleash devastating attacks, but also makes you feel so awesome, due in no small part to the impeccable visual design and feedback behind your actions.

It’s not just the combat that is stylish, too. The entire game oozes with a creativity that truly makes it art. From the menus, the transitions, to even just your footsteps in Palaces, every aspect of the game’s visuals is finely tuned to put a fat grin on your face at all times. Sharp edges to the interface and irregular, asymmetrical shapes all make it fit in beautifully with the rebellious theme of the game. It also perfects its use of colour and design of characters – everything just works so well and makes it contender for the most pleasing game to look at full stop.

The overall gameplay loop of Persona 5 Royal is deceptively simple. Working on calendar days, you have set number of free slots of time every day to do something. You can talk to your friends, get a part time job, train with your team, or infiltrate a Palace. Each Palace is a large, dungeon-style location that you must fully navigate your way through before a certain date, your deadline. While that all sounds so fine and dandy, there is a LOT you can do in your free time. There are movie theatres, books, video games, restaurants, hang out spots, and a massive variety of people to build your bonds with. Unless you’re following some perfect schedule guide, you can’t do everything. With so much to do, you have to plan your time accordingly to make sure you’re both strong enough to take on whatever comes your way, and bonded enough with your confidants to get their perks and abilities, as well as experiencing their stories, most of which are highly engaging, with a couple of exceptions (I’m talking about you, Ohya). And yes I romanced Makoto she is the best.

In terms of the design for each Palace, as well as Mementos, is concerned – most are great. Due to the randomised nature of Mementos, it can feel a little repetitive or uninteresting if you’re just grinding it out or spamming between areas to check for stamps. Of course, some Palaces are better than others too and there isn’t really a definitive, universally agreed upon ranking of them. Most of them I would consider greatly enjoyable, but there are a couple in particular that feel as if they have a little less depth than others, and one key Palace that to me felt like a chore to complete, primarily due to it being stretched out a little more than necessary and having the worst mechanic out of the lot. That said, I still enjoyed the setting and structure (for the most part) of this Palace, which may put to perspective the high standard of the rest of them. 


## Don't Forget the Soundtrack
---

Even if you haven’t played Persona 5 or Royal, chances are you’ve probably heard some of its music, even if you haven’t realised it. This game’s soundtrack is straight FIRE. It is by far and away one of, if not the greatest of all time. Even the songs that are low key and playing in simpler, less important sections of the game are amazing. But when it needs to, this game goes all out and does not hold back. It has fantastic vibes and gets you grooving even while you’re getting your backside handed to you by a particularly vicious enemy. Songs like *Life Will Change*, *Last Surprise*, *Colors Flying High*, *Whims of Fate*, *Ideal and the Real* and *Hoshi To Bokura To* all still stick with me to this day, and are genuinely parts of some of my playlists. The soundtrack is so fitting for the game’s vibes and heightens all levels of enjoyment. I just LOVE it, peak music there.


## Verdict
---

Out of fear that I am sounding way too pretentious, I’m going to shut up now. I probably haven’t said anything at all useful, but Persona 5 Royal is as close to a perfect game as you can get. Fantastic writing, fantastic story, fantastic voice acting, fantastic music, fantastic gameplay, fantastic everything. Minus a few flaws in cringy fan service moments and a couple minor hitches in pacing, there is very little wrong with this game. I would go as far to say it is a must play.

*Game played on a base PS5 and Nintendo Switch*
